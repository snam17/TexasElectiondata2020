Whole Food Counties Backstory and Process

One way of understanding the vote in Texas is by using the counties that have Whole Foods stores located in them to essentially split the state up into urban and rural areas. This method was popularized by political analyst David Wasserman of the Cook Political Report. Since there were no exisiting data for which county in Texas has the Whole Foods store, we manually created the dataset based on the Whole Foods store locator (found at https://www.wholefoodsmarket.com/stores) combined with Google Maps.

The cities and counties that Whole Foods are located in Texas are as follows: El Paso (El Paso County), San Antonio (Bexar County), Austin (Travis and Williamson Counties), Houston (Montgomery, Harris, and Fort Bend Counties), Dallas Fort Worth (Dallas, COllin, Denton, and Tarrant Counties). This is a total of 11 counties. So of the 254 counties in the state of Texas, 243 of them are not “Whole Foods Counties.”

Calculating the Texas Population Inside and Outside of Whole Foods Counties

As we can see from this analysis, 60.42% of the total Texas population lives in one of these 11 counties, while the rest of population (39.58%) lives in one of the 243 counties without a Whole Foods located within its boundaries.

Now that we know the total population in each group, let’s look at some of the other differences like demographic and age group breakdown, as well as early vote percentage.

Early Vote in Whole Foods vs. Non-Whole Foods Counties

While the Whole Foods counties accounted for 60.42% of the total Texas population, they account for 56.76% of the early vote in Texas for the 2020 election, accounting for 5507445 of the 9702659 state-wide early votes.

Interestingly, the non Whole Foods counties outperformed the Whole Foods counties in terms of their early voting numbers, as the non Whole Foods counties accounted for a higher percentage of the early vote compared to their percentage of the overall population.

This is quite interesting, as this story line did not make its way into the pre-election coverage that we saw. In fact, last minute the momentum was swinging in the democrats direction, while the early vote in this instance tells a different story.

Comparing the Racial Demographics of Whole Foods vs. Non-Whole Foods Counties

As shown on the charts, there is a clear difference in demographics between the counties with Whole Foods and the counties without a Whole Foods location. The counties without a Whole Foods tend to have a higher percentage of whites and lower percentage of African Americans and Asian Americans. As seen in an earlier step suggested that the hispanic population was within one percent for the two groups.

When comparing the two age plots side by side, it is clear that there are differences in the distribution of the ages between the two groups of counties. When looking at the non-Whole Foods counties, the only time a group reaches 10% of the voting age population is for group 5. However, when looking at the Whole Foods counties, group 5 is below 10%, while groups 6 through 8 are over 10%. This suggests that the Whole Foods generally have a higher percentage of young people than the non Whole Foods counties.
